TrainQuest

A playful training companion for university students who want to build exercise habits through small daily missions, visible progress, and motivating rewards.

Mission Brief

We are designing a human-centred web experience that turns training from a vague, easily-abandoned task into a structured and rewarding routine. Our focus is not professional coach management, but habit-building for students and beginners.

Current Focus

Discovering requirements through personas, journey mapping, and identifying where motivation drops during the exercise journey.

Why This Matters

Many students want to exercise more regularly, but they face three recurring barriers: unclear starting points, weak short-term motivation, and poor visibility of progress. TrainQuest explores how playful interaction design can reduce those barriers.

🧭

Clarity

Users should know what to do today without scrolling through complicated plans.

🎯

Motivation

Small wins, visible rewards, and streaks can make training feel worth continuing.

📈

Growth

Progress should be easy to see so users feel that effort turns into achievement.

Design Direction

Our Working Principles

  • Make the first step obvious.
  • Use playful feedback without making the system childish.
  • Break large fitness goals into short, achievable daily quests.
  • Show progress in a way that feels encouraging rather than pressuring.

Target Users

Instead of designing for “everyone who exercises,” we focus on students and beginner users who want support, structure, and motivation.

Experience Journey

We mapped the exercise journey to identify where user motivation falls and where playful support could create better continuity.

1. Intention

User action: Decides to start exercising.

Feeling: Hopeful but uncertain.

Opportunity: Provide a simple “today’s quest” entry point.

2. Exploration

User action: Looks at workouts and compares options.

Feeling: Curious but overwhelmed.

Opportunity: Reduce choice overload with guided recommendations.

3. Early Use

User action: Completes the first few tasks.

Feeling: Motivated for a short period.

Opportunity: Reinforce momentum through XP, badges, and completion feedback.

4. Friction

User action: Starts missing sessions and delaying workouts.

Feeling: Frustrated and disengaged.

Opportunity: Use streaks, reminders, and lighter daily missions to reduce drop-off.

5. Habit or Drop-off

User action: Either continues the routine or gives up.

Feeling: Proud if progress is visible; disappointed if effort feels invisible.

Opportunity: Visualise milestones and weekly growth summaries.

Core Feature Concepts

🗓️

Daily Quest

A short and clear task set that gives users an immediate starting point for the day.

🏅

Reward Loop

XP, badges, and streaks that turn completion into visible positive reinforcement.

🧩

Progress Trail

A compact dashboard that helps users recognise growth over time rather than only daily effort.

Research Plan

To avoid designing based only on assumptions, we plan to collect user insights through lightweight interviews, short questionnaires, and competitor review. This aligns with our requirement discovery stage and helps us validate whether motivation, clarity, and progress visibility are the real barriers in student exercise habits.

🎤

Interviews

Conduct short semi-structured interviews with university students to understand how they currently exercise, why they stop, and what kind of feedback feels motivating.

📋

Questionnaire

Use a short survey to identify common frustrations such as lack of time, lack of structure, and low motivation after the first few sessions.

🔎

Competitor Review

Review existing fitness and habit-tracking apps to compare how they present daily tasks, rewards, reminders, and progress tracking.

Initial Findings

Early Hypotheses We Want to Validate

  • Users are more likely to start exercising if the first task is very small and clearly defined.
  • Motivation drops when users cannot immediately see progress or reward.
  • Too much choice creates confusion, especially for beginner users.
  • Playful feedback can reduce pressure and make training feel more sustainable.

Design Opportunities

Based on our personas and experience journey, we identified several opportunities where design intervention could improve continuity, clarity, and motivation.

Opportunity 1 · Reduce Starting Friction

Instead of asking users to create a full training plan, TrainQuest should immediately present one recommended “Today’s Quest” so users can begin without overthinking.

Opportunity 2 · Reward Small Wins

The system should celebrate small completions with XP, badges, and micro-feedback so users feel progress even before long-term fitness changes become visible.

Opportunity 3 · Make Progress Visible

A compact dashboard should help users see streaks, completed sessions, and milestone growth so that effort feels accumulated rather than forgotten.

Opportunity 4 · Keep the Tone Encouraging

The interface should sound supportive and playful, not overly strict or intimidating, especially for students and beginner users.
